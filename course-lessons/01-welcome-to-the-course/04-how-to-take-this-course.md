# How to Take This Course

There are a few different ways to approach this course, and I want to be upfront about that so you can get the most out of it.

## The Core Goal

My primary objective is to give you a repeatable AI development workflow. In most areas of software development, there are established patterns — specific methods, commands, design patterns. With AI-assisted development, there aren't. Ask ten developers how they manage their AI workflow and you'll get ten completely different answers.

I want to change that for you. The first half of this course is dedicated to building a strict, structured workflow: adding features one at a time using custom commands, managing Git, documenting your work, implementing, testing, iterating, and reviewing — all while keeping your context under control. You can have that workflow fully internalized by the halfway point.

## The Two Ways to Take It

**Option 1: Learn the workflow and move on.** You don't need to watch every video or follow along through the entire project. If your goal is to walk away with a solid AI development process you can apply to your own work, you can get that in the first half and be done.

**Option 2: Follow the full build.** The second half of the course is the workflow applied in practice — building DevStash from scratch and deploying it, the same way I'd do it off-camera on my own. I narrate what the model is doing throughout: what files and components it's creating, why we're making certain architectural decisions, how we run migrations, and how we audit the output. If you want 16 hours of that, it's there.

Both are valid. Find what works for you.

## One Thing to Keep in Mind

If you do follow along with the full build, your result will not be identical to mine — even if you use the same tools, the same prompts, and the same feature spec files. You may end up with a different architecture, different patterns, a slightly different UI. That's expected. This isn't like my React or PHP courses where you write the same code line by line and land on the same project. There's far less hand-holding here, by design.

That's not a flaw — it's the nature of working with AI. Embrace it.
